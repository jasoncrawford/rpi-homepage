Contemporary society has lost faith in progress, contrasting sharply with the optimistic philosophy of the Enlightenment era.

## Historical Context

The Enlightenment fostered a philosophy centered on progress. Marquis de Condorcet articulated this vision in his _Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind_, predicting advancement not only in technology but also in morality and social equality. Though Condorcet was executed during the French Revolution, material progress accelerated dramatically.

The 19th century witnessed extraordinary technological achievements—railroads, telephones, light bulbs, and engines—leading naturalist Alfred Russel Wallace to call it "The Wonderful Century." Many believed technological advancement would eventually produce moral progress, potentially ending war and poverty.

## The 20th Century Reversal

World War I and II shattered these optimistic assumptions. "The world wars were devastating proof that material progress does not inevitably lead to moral progress." The atomic bomb symbolized how scientific achievement could produce unprecedented destruction.

This disillusionment spawned skepticism toward progress itself. Growth became characterized as an "addiction" and progress as a "fairy tale," fueling movements advocating degrowth and technological restraint. Consequently, innovation stagnated—nuclear power development slowed, space exploration halted, and major projects ceased.

## A Path Forward

The answer lies neither in naive 19th-century optimism nor 20th-century rejection of progress. Rather, society requires a balanced philosophy acknowledging progress's genuine benefits while honestly confronting its challenges and consequences.
