Crawford proposes three foundational premises for a modern progress movement: progress as historical fact, humanism as moral standard, and human agency in shaping the future.

## Progress as Historical Fact

The essay begins by establishing that material living standards have dramatically improved over the past 200 years—what McCloskey terms "the Great Fact." This observation motivates progress studies. Crawford contrasts this with declinism, which argues society has deteriorated despite technological advancement, questioning whether "agriculture was a mistake" or suggesting that contemporary life is worse than hunter-gatherer existence.

## Humanism as the Standard of Value

Humanism, as Crawford defines it, measures progress by its contribution to human flourishing: "longer, healthier, happier lives; lives of more choice and opportunity." This encompasses not just material needs but psychological ones including "excitement, adventure, romance, beauty, knowledge, exploration, and human connection."

The author identifies romantic opposition to humanism from both political directions—environmental romanticism from the left and traditionalist concerns from the right. Humanist philosophy prioritizes human wellbeing over environmental preservation or cultural preservation when conflicts arise.

## A Belief in Human Agency

Agency represents the conviction that "our future is shaped by our choices and actions." Crawford deliberately avoids "optimism," distinguishing between prescriptive optimism (philosophical attitude) and descriptive optimism (factual prediction). He proposes "solutionist" as preferable terminology.

Fatalism opposes agency, manifesting as beliefs about uncontrollable complexity, resource depletion inevitably halting progress, or technological stagnation. Crawford argues that progress, while creating "new problems," generates "better ones to have."

## Political Neutrality and Future Debate

Crawford emphasizes these three ideas intentionally exclude explicit political positions, inviting dialogue across libertarian and progressive viewpoints. He identifies remaining philosophical questions requiring "healthy debates": the extent of historical improvement, treatment of war and inequality, definitions of human wellbeing, aggregation of individual interests, and animal welfare considerations.

The essay concludes by connecting these premises to Cowen and Collison's "progress studies" framework, arguing that progress, humanism, and agency are necessary to motivate understanding progress's causes and accelerating it.
